Short Bio

David Wood is a Ph.D. candidate at The University of Chicago, affiliated with the Econometrics and Statistics Area at Booth School of Business. He works on formal aspects of time series with smooth interactive dynamics, emphasizing on integration with modern mathematics and physics methods. As Einstein says, "Education is what remains after one has forgotten everything he learned in school": David holds on to the tradition of viewing doctoral education as noble intellectual exploration with the aim of dialectical transcendence and the organic extension of his liberal arts education at The University of Chicago College. After completing the core curriculum at the Department of Economics in his first year, along continuous training in his home field of financial econometrics, David went on to finish the Ph.D. Mathematics Core (year-long sequences in Analysis, Algebra, and Geometry) and Ph.D. Physics Core (year-long sequences in Quantum Field Theory and String Theory) as well as dozens of specialized seminars in representation, harmonic, and field theories, a first in Chicago history, but faithful to its motto.

As the custodian to a real estate company in China, David transforms his business education at Booth into analytical leadership in the emerging market. Under his management, the company won a landmark case at the Chinese Supreme Court and helped to redefine the property rights in China. David is also the founder of a proprietary trading company specialized in oil futures along with two classmates at The University of Chicago College.

David grew up in a small city in the South China and his Chinese name is 吴小龙 (pronunciation). David worked on his B.A. paper under the supervision of Prof Roger Myerson and graduated from the College with a B.A. in Economics. He is the first person in his family to attend college. David plays the piano, with a repertoire of Baroque, Classical, and early-Romantic music.

Mathematical Underpinnings of Hilbert-Huang Transform

Abstract. The Hilbert-Huang Transform (HHT) applies the Hilbert spectral analysis on a set of the intrinsic mode functions, obtained through the sifting process. Though the method has established itself as an empirically useful alternative to the Fourier Transform, the mathematical nature of the HHT is not well understood. Utilizing the harmonic theory, I describe and characterize the HHT methodology rigorously from the perspective of the Hilbert space. In the process, I discuss the theoretical limits of the HHT method.

The Yang–Mills Existence and Mass Gap Problem

Summary. Since the close form characterizations of a Yang-Mills theory on a non-compact 4-dimensional space seem unlikely, the efforts of the past half-century focus on producing converging sequences of approximations to the desired QFT: this implicit strategy explains the overall framing of the problem in terms of functional analysis. I argue the bare-bone axioms established through analysis are useful to flash out the theoretical features desired, to truly encounter the problem, we need to develop new local geometries compatible with these axiomatic features. To accomplish this, I first give a quick review of what we have learned from the Standard Model. The elementary QCD phenomenology seems to provide adequate motivation to the problem. I then sketch out the classical gauge theory with the language of algebraic geometry. In the last parts, I first introduce the axiomatic properties required by the problem and then reflect on our strategies to study the problem further as a beginner.

Time-Varying Cross-Correlations in Stock Returns

Abstract. By Sharpe (1964) and Lintner's (1965) Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM), stock returns are correlated with the market returns. As a consequence, there must be nontrivial correlation structures among stock returns. I study the cross-correlations in stock returns over time. Using S&P 500 stocks as an example, I show existing conditional heteroscedasticity models fail due to high dimensionality and the inclusion of the financial crisis period of the 2008, characterized by irregular price movements. I develop new statistical tools to study the time-varying patterns of cross-correlations in stock returns. I use these tools to demonstrate structural changes to industry correlations and market correlations during the financial crisis.

Towards Clustering-Based Trading Strategies

Abstract. I experiment with a new trading strategy based on dynamic affinity propagation clustering. I empirically evaluate the strategy with intraday data of 140 most actively traded stocks in the summer against two benchmarks; trading stocks individually and trading stocks based on static industry blocks. Dynamic clustering increases accumulative trading profit by 35.4% and 5.6%, comparing to the two benchmarks respectively, while decreasing the standard deviation of daily P&Ls by 10.3% and 20.8% respectively.
